40.2 Comparing Thai Documents

Similar to Arabic projects, Thai projects should be started with the appropriate Complex Languages project settings. This makes it possible to deal with some specialties of the script:

40.2.1 Spacing Rules

In Thai, word spacing has no meaning. Sometimes manuscripts contain white spaces between words but artwork documents do not. White spaces are mostly used to separate full sentences.

TVT uses the Unicode Standard for separating Thai words, but the presence or absence of spaces will still be detected as a spacing warning. As Thai does not use uppercase/lowercase letters, interword spaces will always cause major spacing warnings. These may be turned off in the Map Warnings Dialog, but this will cause all other major spacing warnings to be hidden.

40.2.2 Justified Text

As part of the alignment process, needle spaces may be introduced between each glyph. This is especially the case when the text column is narrow and there are no sentence separating spaces on the line. If the spaces reach a certain value, TVT will interpret them as major spacing warnings.

40.2.3 Combining Characters

Most Thai characters consist of a series of code points (please see chapter 33.1.1 "Combining Character Sequences" on page 1). In rare cases, the order of these code points will be swapped. This may or may not change the appearance of the glyph and must be inspected manually. The relationship between glyph and code points can be seen best in the Compare Selection Area when selecting a glyph. Every code point contained in the glyph will be listed there.

The Unicode Standard states that combining characters in Thai should be ordered from inside to outside. Sometimes the text extraction from PDF documents may not be exact and swap the combining characters.

40.2.4 Sara Am Ligature

The Sara Am is a precomposed character that also combines with the previous glyph. As there are different Unicode notations that will deliver the same glyph appearance this may raise deviations:

When used inside Sara Am, the Nikhahit will combine with the previous glyph in a way that it will be displayed directly at the base character. As a result, different representations of identical content are possible:

No Nu (U+E19) Nikhahit (U+0E4D)  Mai Tho (U+0E49) Sara Aa (U+0E32)

is equivalent to

No Nu (U+E19) Mai Tho (U+0E49) Sara Am (U+0E33)

These ligature transitions are detected as deviations and have to be compared manually. When the Sara Am ligature is used in InDesign, this may lead to a replacement character (U+FFFD) directly following the ligature.
