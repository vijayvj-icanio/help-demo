23.15 Comparing Arabic and Hebrew Documents

There are some challenges specific to Arabic and Hebrew documents. These originate mostly from a mixture of right-to-left (RTL) text with left-to-right (LTR) insertions, e.g. pharmaceutical ingredients or brand names.

Global project settings (e.g. “Leaflets (Complex Languages)”) apply a set of normalization rules to PDF documents that reduce the number of deviations introduced through the PDF creation process.

The following issues are currently known:

23.15.1 Brackets

Brackets belong to the Unicode characters that have a “mirroring” property: They appear differently, usually mirror-inverted, when the Unicode bidirectional rules deem it appropriate, depending on the surrounding text.

Thus, in bidirectional contexts, left (opening) and right (closing) brackets can be interpreted differently in MS Word and Adobe PDF documents. For example, in Word, an opening bracket has the Unicode U+0028 and appears as “(” in LTR text, but as “)” in RTL text.

Mismatched brackets in RTL text will be caught and displayed as Bracket Flip Warnings. For more details, please check See Special Topics "Bracket Flip Category" on page 1 Bracket Flip and Math Flip Categories

23.15.2 LTR Insertions

When RTL text is inserted into LTR text, Word and PDF documents have certain specialties that affect screen display and also text extraction.

Word uses an undocumented text direction algorithm that is different from the Unicode Bidirectional Algorithm. This sometimes makes it difficult to gather the intended text ordering. As a result, text within or close to LTR insertions may not appear in the same order as in Microsoft Word. Since numbers in Arabic text are to be read from left to right, numbering in Arabic is a typical case of a LTR insertion that may be equivocally displayed in RTL order.

In PDF documents, the success of the text extraction is based on the quality of the PDF creation process. Especially in RTL insertions, the reading order may differ from the text extraction order resulting in additional order breaks and deviations.

23.15.3 Kasheda Justification

In Arabic, this special justification is used in a way similar to the regular justification. As spaces are not extended here, so called Tatweel characters (similar to underscore) are inserted into the words to extend the line width to the required size. With InDesign, the Tatweels are inserted outside the reading order creating insertions and sometimes preventing the mapping of matched text. Also, Tatweel characters may be badly encoded by the replacement character U+FFFD.

23.15.4 Arabic Fonts

Each character has four different appearances, depending upon whether there are spaces to the left or right of it. Since those appearances are not encoded into the Unicode code point, it is vital that both the font and applications do not contain flaws.

As a general rule, comparing bidirectional text requires much more manual work than comparing European documents. It is also recommended that the operator have an understanding of the language so that differences between display in TVT and MS Word can be identified.
